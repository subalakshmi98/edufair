Rural children in developing countries face many problems in getting to and staying in school. In addition to well-known social and economic factors, distance-related obstacles to schooling are very important but surprisingly disregarded in the available literature. This paper analyzes the present situation in rural Brazil and specially in the state of São Paulo, where a large rural school transport system has been operated since the 1960s. The analysis considers mainly policy issues as a guide to the provision of rural pupil transport. The analysis considers also the advantages and limitations of different strategies for improving the access to schools in rural areas of developing countries. An example of pupil transport coupled to rural school consolidation is provided. Finally, a preliminary framework for the definition of a specific public policy is proposed. The relationship of distance and schooling is particularly critical in rural areas, where children must walk long distances to widely dispersed schools and must travel additional distances to nearby urban areas for higher primary grades. With the lack of public transportation and an inability to pay for private transportation, most rural children abandon the school system after relatively few years. This distance-related problem concerns the analysis of accessibility - both time and physical accessibility - (Moseley et al., 1977), as well as the issues of welfare and equity in the access to public services. The access to school is an important part of accessibility to essential activities, which have to be considered as a right in contemporary life. If convenient transport means are available to support this right, the measured results reflect the degree of ‘transport-related welfare’, while the opposite reflects a condition of ‘transport-induced deprivation’ (Nutley, 1984, p 358). Hence, transport infrastructure and transport means play a vital role in attaining the basic needs of the rural population. Safety in pupil transport is of paramount relevance. It is related to several feaures of the transport system, mainly the driver capability and vehicle and road conditions. In the case of specialized services, bus drivers are key elements of the system, both operationally and socially. They are normally chosen in the local market and have natural linkages to local people, which helps to foster responsible behavior. Nevertheless, most school drivers do not receive adequate training and sometimes drive unsafely. School bus drivers in the state of SCo Paulo must meet special qualifications: a minimum age of 21, possession of a professional driver’s licence, and have received special training. The present vehicles can be considered adequate with the exception of the ‘kombi’, which is not well equipped to transport children. In Sgo Paulo, vehicles have to have special devices like a speedometer and external safety signs and lights and have to pass through physical evaluations twice a year. However, deficient enforcement allows unskilled or ill-prepared drivers to keep working in remote areas, where it is also common to see dilapidated vehicles. With respect to roads, travel on gravel roads occurs at low speeds and accidents used to be rare events. Nevertheless, when paved roads are used, vehicles travel at more dangerous speeds and the existence of intersections require careful planning: the use of such roads in SLo Paula has led to fatal accidents.
